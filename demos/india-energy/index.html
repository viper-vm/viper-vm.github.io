Can India be called “energy-rich”?

It depends entirely on which energy you mean. India is two countries at once: hydrocarbon-poor and renewable/coal-rich.

Oil — poor. 672 MT proved reserves, ~23 years at current output, declining fields. India will import ~85–90% of its crude for the foreseeable future.
Gas — modest, with upside. ~1,073 BCM proved + large unexplored deepwater (KG, Andaman, Mahanadi). Still ~50% import-dependent today.
Coal — rich. ~5th-largest reserves on Earth (~350+ BT geological); ~1 BT mined a year. India's true fossil endowment.
Solar & wind — extraordinarily rich. 3,343 GW feasible solar potential (NISE 2025) and 765 GW wind (NIWE). This is where “energy-rich” is genuinely true.

The honest one-liner

India is fossil-fuel-import-dependent but renewables-rich. It cannot drill its way to energy independence on oil — the reserves simply aren't there. It can get there on electricity: sun, wind, coal-backed grid, hydro, and a fast-growing nuclear + green-hydrogen layer. The strategic game is therefore electrification (move transport, heat and industry onto a domestic clean grid) far more than exploration.

Exploration still matters — every domestic barrel saves hard currency and a ~$120–140 bn/yr oil import bill — but it shrinks the import problem, it doesn't eliminate it.

Exploration & recent discoveries

Now producing

KG-DWN-98/2 (Cluster-2)

ONGC's flagship ultra-deepwater project off the Godavari delta. First oil flowed in Jan 2024; by Oct 2024, three A-field wells lifted output to ~25,000 bopd across 8 wells, heading to a peak around 45,000 boe/d + gas. ~₹40,000 cr / $5 bn programme — India's hardest engineering job in the ocean.

Frontier bet

Andaman ultra-deepwater

The “could-be-Guyana” gamble. ONGC + OIL are drilling to ~5,000 m for the first time. Wildcat ANDW-7 in the East Andaman back-arc returned traces of light crude & condensate in cuttings — encouraging, but not yet a commercial discovery. If it hits, first oil is a decade-plus, $10 bn-scale journey.

Policy unlock

OALP + “No-Go” areas opened

Only ~10% of India's sedimentary basin area was under active exploration; the government has been releasing previously off-limits (“No-Go”) defence/forest acreage and auctioning blocks via OALP rounds, aiming to push exploration acreage up sharply and bring Category-II/III basins into play.

Reality check on capacity: ONGC's KG cluster holds an estimated ~69.6 BCM gas in place (of which ~51 BCM is producible over 2018–34) plus oil — large for India, but a rounding error against the ~243 MT of crude imported every year. Frontier deepwater is the only thing on the map with the scale to move the needle, and it's the least certain.

What does it cost — and is it worth getting out?

Approx. break-even to lift a barrel

Indicative all-in production cost, US$/barrel of oil equivalent. Lower = more feasible. (Brent for scale ≈ $70–85.)

The feasibility logic

  • Onshore & shallow offshore (~$25–40/bbl): clearly economic at any normal oil price — but these fields are mature and declining, so the prize is slowing the decline, not growth.
  • Deepwater (~$45–60/bbl): economic when oil > ~$55–60, which is the bet behind KG cluster-2. Sensitive to price crashes.
  • Ultra-deepwater frontier (~$60–80+/bbl, est.): only worth it if the find is big (billion-barrel class). A small Andaman pool would never pay back the rigs and pipelines.
  • The renewables twist: Indian solar is among the world's cheapest electricity (record tariffs near ₹2/kWh, LCOE roughly $25–35/MWh). For power, building solar is cheaper and faster than chasing deep oil — which is exactly why exploration is a hedge, not the main plan.

The next frontier: minerals & nuclear fuel

Critical minerals (the energy-transition fuels)

  • Lithium — Reasi, J&K: 5.9 MT inferred (2023), at the time ~7th largest globally. But it's clay-hosted — extraction is unproven and first auctions failed. Now being re-explored to G2 level.
  • Lithium — Mandya, Karnataka: ~1,600 t inferred — small, early.
  • Why it matters: batteries decide whether India's solar/wind can run at night and power EVs without importing cells from China.

Nuclear — the thorium card

  • Uranium: Jaduguda (Jharkhand) and Tummalapalle (Andhra Pradesh) — modest reserves; India still imports uranium.
  • Thorium: India holds one of the world's largest thorium reserves in the monazite beach sands of Kerala & Tamil Nadu — the long-term bet behind India's 3-stage nuclear programme.
  • Status: ~8 GW nuclear today, expanding; thorium reactors are still developmental, a multi-decade horizon.
